Team houses was my team for the "Flagship" Olin class, User Oriented Collaborative Design (UOCD). The whole idea of UOCD is to approach product design from the perspective of the user. Each team is given a "user group"; a group of people with something in common that is not their profession. It is then the team's job to learn everything there is to know about that group, both their common attributes and the interests of the individuals. From this knowledge it is possible for the team to pull out common values that will allow the team to design that will have the highest positive impact on the group. 
The user group of Team houses was "people who live in tiny houses" (yay for visual puns). After dozens of hours of user interviews and visits, were able to boil down our core user values to: Sustainability, Intentionality, Affordability, and Accessibility. Once there it was a simple matter of generating hundreds (quite literally) of product ideas--most of which were physically impossible according to everything we know about physics--and bringing the best ones down into reality. After probably over 200 hours, we completed the project with the Spirit Staircase.
The Spirit Staircase is a ladder reminiscent of Lapeyre Staircase allowing for easy access to the lofts so common in tiny houses. More extraordinary is the ability of the second to last step to fold out in front and act as a seat that will carry the mobility impaired up and down. Also the Spirit Staircase, when not in use, will lift itself  out of the way. 

​Although the impact on the Tiny House community might not be immediately apparent--after all, most members of the community are relatively young fit individuals. The value in the Spirit Staircase is its ability to open up Tiny House living to a much larger group including older people that are looking to downsize (a larger number of which we found on tiny house forums). So what? Tiny Houses are technically illegal and current members of the community believe that the best way to change that is with a large grassroots movement, but that will never happen if members are limited to recent college graduates.
